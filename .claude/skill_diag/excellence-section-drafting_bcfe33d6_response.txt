They produce plans that degrade unpredictably over long task horizons, lose coherence across extended interactions, fail silently in novel situations, cannot reliably coordinate with other agents, and lack principled mechanisms for integrating external tools and APIs into their reasoning loops.       "content": "The MAESTRO methodology is organised around three co-designed research pillars unified by a cross-cutting External Tool and API Orchestration Layer, validated in operational settings through three demonstrators co-designed with industry partners, and integrated and benchmarked through a dedicated cross-cutting work package. The overriding methodological principle is co-design from project inception: all three pillars share common data representations, evaluation benchmarks, and integration interfaces established at the start of the project, enabling genuine architectural integration rather than post-hoc assembly of independent research threads. This design choice directly addresses the fragmentation of the current research landscape as identified in the state-of-the-art analysis.\n\nPillar 1 — Neuro-Symbolic Planning and Reasoning (WP2, Lead: ATU): The methodological core of Pillar 1 is the hybridisation of symbolic planning methods — with their formal guarantees of correctness and completeness — and neural LLM-based reasoning — with its flexibility in handling natural language ambiguity and open-ended task specifications. WP2 develops this through three sequential tasks. Task T2-01 (sub-task graph decomposition design) formalises goal decomposition as a structured planning problem, specifying pre-conditions, post-conditions, and verification checkpoints as formal constraints. Task T2-02 (MCTS and HTN planning integration with LLM heuristics) integrates Monte Carlo Tree Search for online planning under uncertainty and hierarchical task networks for hierarchical goal decomposition with LLM-driven heuristic evaluation that leverages neural reasoning to guide symbolic search efficiently through combinatorially large plan spaces. Task T2-03 (plan monitoring and self-correction implementation) implements execution failure detection against verification checkpoints and replanning from the most recent verified intermediate state, avoiding full plan restarts. The central methodological assumption is that LLM-based heuristic guidance, when constrained by formal pre/post-conditions, produces substantially higher plan success rates than unconstrained chain-of-thought reasoning — a hypothesis grounded in the current body of neuro-symbolic AI research and validated in WP8 against standard agent benchmarks (WebArena, SWE-bench, GAIA). Deliverable D2-01 (neuro-symbolic planning architecture specification, Month 18) and D2-02 (planning engine software prototype, Month 36) are the primary Pillar 1 outputs.\n\nPillar 2 — Adaptive Memory Architecture (WP3, Lead: BIIS): The methodological core of Pillar 2 addresses the fundamental limitation that effective long-horizon agent behaviour requires memory extending beyond the context window of any single LLM call. The three-store memory architecture provides: working memory — a structured, attention-managed short-term store maintaining the agent's current goals, active plan state, and recent observations; episodic memory — a retrieval-augmented store of past task episodes indexed by context similarity, enabling experience-based behaviour without model retraining; and semantic memory — a persistent knowledge graph connecting domain concepts, tool capabilities, and learned task patterns, continuously updated through agent interactions. WP3 develops this through three tasks: T3-01 (tri-store memory architecture design), T3-02 (memory indexing and retrieval strategy implementation, building directly on BIIS's established expertise in retrieval-augmented generation and neural information retrieval), and T3-03 (forgetting mechanism and coherence maintenance, addressing the computational cost and memory staleness challenges inherent in persistent long-horizon memory systems). The memory controller manages information flow between stores, deciding what to commit to long-term memory, what to retrieve, and when to forget. Deliverable D3-01 (memory architecture specification, Month 12) and D3-02 (adaptive memory system software, Month 30) are the primary Pillar 2 outputs.\n\nPillar 3 — Decentralised Multi-Agent Coordination (WP4, Lead: CERIA): The methodological core of Pillar 3 is grounded in CERIA's expertise in game theory, mechanism design, and formal verification of multi-agent protocols. Task T4-01 (task-delegation and negotiation protocol design) develops a formal task-delegation protocol using formal specification techniques to define agent commitment structures, negotiation semantics, and termination conditions with provable convergence properties. Task T4-02 (convergence and conflict resolution under partial observability) addresses a central challenge of distributed multi-agent operation: agents that operate with incomplete world models may develop inconsistent plans or contest shared resources. Task T4-03 (auditable multi-agent interaction trace module) implements structured recording of every delegation, negotiation step, and joint decision for human inspection — a capability with direct relevance to accountability and safety in real-world deployments. This approach differs methodologically from ad-hoc LLM-based agent communication patterns precisely by providing formally provable convergence — a distinction critical for safety-sensitive healthcare and manufacturing deployments. Deliverable D4-01 (coordination protocol specification, Month 18) and D4-02 (decentralised coordination software, Month 36) are the primary Pillar 3 outputs.\n\nCross-Cutting Capability — External Tool and API Orchestration Layer: The External Tool and API Orchestration Layer spans all three research pillars, with development primarily within WP2 (planning integration) and WP3 (memory integration for tool execution history and learned tool-selection heuristics). The typed tool registry provides machine-readable tool capability models — including input/output schemas, pre-conditions, post-conditions, cost profiles, and reliability metadata — that agents can query, reason over, and update. Formal tool selection and invocation policies, integrated with the neuro-symbolic planning engine (T2-02), determine when and why to invoke external tools based on task requirements, expected accuracy gains, and cost and latency trade-offs. Standardised API adapters abstract heterogeneous external protocols — REST, GraphQL, HL7 FHIR in healthcare, OPC-UA in manufacturing — into a uniform invocation interface. Post-invocation verification mechanisms validate tool outputs against expected schemas and domain constraints before incorporating results into agent state, grounding agent reasoning in verified external data. Execution monitoring with timeout detection, error classification, and automated fallback strategies is integrated with T2-03 plan self-correction. The key methodological contribution is architecturally integrating tool selection within the planning loop — not as a post-hoc function-calling interface — such that the planning engine formally reasons about when and why to invoke external tools as part of task planning.\n\nValidation and Demonstrators: All three research pillars are validated in operational settings through demonstrators co-designed with industry partners who provide domain expertise, deployment environments, and operational data. WP5 (Lead: NIHS) deploys a clinical decision support system integrating MAESTRO with HL7 FHIR clinical data APIs, validated against NIHS's de-identified clinical data repository (3M+ patient records, ethics-approved) and NIHS's clinical simulation environment against real diagnostic pathways. WP7 (Lead: ELI) deploys an agent-based logistics planning system across three international supply chain corridors on ELI's operational logistics data and supply chain simulation environment. MAESTRO's research pillars build directly on these foundations — hybrid AI architectures, formal multi-agent protocols, neural retrieval systems, clinical AI validation methodologies, manufacturing AI validation environments, and EU AI ecosystem infrastructure — and feeds new unified results back into these initiatives.\n\nInterdisciplinary Approach: MAESTRO is inherently interdisciplinary. The core research integrates artificial intelligence (neuro-symbolic planning, LLM-based reasoning, multi-agent systems), formal methods (protocol specification, convergence verification, pre/post-condition logic from ATU and CERIA), information retrieval and machine learning (neural memory indexing and retrieval from BIIS), and software and systems engineering (API integration, distributed systems engineering from ATU and BAL).
